Description

Note: This is the second AIMO Progress Prize competition. It builds upon the first AIMO Progress Prize competition, which was won in July 2024 by Project Numina. This second competition has an increased prize pool, a new dataset of problems, increased compute for participants and updated rules for using open-source LLMs.

The ability to reason mathematically is a critical milestone for AI. Mathematical reasoning is the foundation for solving many complex problems, from engineering marvels to intricate financial models. However, current AI capabilities are limited in this area.

The AI Mathematical Olympiad (AIMO) Prize is a $10mn fund to spur the open development of AI models capable of performing as well as top human participants in the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO).

This second AIMO Progress Prize competition has 110 math problems in algebra, combinatorics, geometry and number theory. The difficulty has been increased from the first competition, and the problems are now around the National Olympiad level. The problems have also been designed to be 'AI hard' in terms of the mathematical reasoning required, which was tested against current open LLMs' capabilities.

To address the challenge of train-test leakage, the competition uses novel math problems created by an international team of problem solvers. Using this transparent and fair evaluation framework, the competition will help to strengthen the benchmarks for assessing AI models' mathematical reasoning skills, without the risk of contamination from training data.
This latest AIMO Progress Prize competition offers an exciting opportunity to drive innovation in the field of AI for Math, while also fostering healthy competition and supporting open science.

Join us as we work towards a future where AI models’ mathematical reasoning skills are accurately and reliably assessed, driving progress and innovation.

Evaluation

Submissions are evaluated on the accuracy between their predicted labels and the ground-truth labels. In other words, submissions are ranked by the fraction of predicted labels that exactly match the ground-truth labels.

In this competition, every ground-truth label is an integer between 0 and 999, inclusive.

You should arrive at this number by taking the problem solution modulo 1000. If, for instance, you believe the solution to a problem is 65521 should be reported as 521 and -900 should be reported as 100. To be clear, for positive integers larger than 1000, this means: report the last three digits, discarding any initial zero(s). Thus 1009 should be reported as 9.

If a question asks for an answer aa to be calculated modulo mm where mm is specified (not all questions are of this type), then calculate the residue aa modulo mm which is a′a′ with 0≤a′<m0≤a′<m and then report this answer modulo 10001000. For example, if asked to calculate the positive integer 20252025 modulo 999999, the final answer should be 2727. However, if asked to calculate 20252025 modulo 10131013, the final answer should be 1212.

Answers may require basic computations, e.g., ⌊1002–√⌋=141⌊1002⌋=141.

Submitting

You must submit to this competition using the provided Python evaluation API, which serves test set instances one-by-one in random order for the public leaderboard, and uses fixed, random order for the private leaderboard. To use the API, follow the template in this notebook.

Note the requirement that the prediction server must be called within 15 minutes of the submission notebook starting, and that each prediction must be returned within 30 minutes.

Note on Language and Notation

All problems are text-only with mathematical notation in LaTeX. The following conventions are observed:

  • amssymbamssymb, amsmathamsmath and enumitemenumitem packages are used. No other package is assumed.
  • Variable names are given in math mode e.g. aa, xx, yy.
  • Integers such as 1, 2 can be outside of math mode or inside. Large integers such as 10501050 are in math mode.
  • itemizeitemize and enumerateenumerate environments may be used (with the latter using the conventions from the enumitemenumitem package).
  • Other environments such as \begin{equation}\begin{equation} are not used.
  • $…$$…$, \ (…\ )\ (…\ ), \[…\]\[…\] may all be used for equations.
  • \ldots\ldots, \cdots\cdots, …… can be used to continue a finite sequence or infinite.
  • Multiplication can be represented by simple adjacency 2x2x or using LaTeX \cdot\cdot command for x⋅yx⋅y or LaTeX \times\times command for x×yx×y.
  • n!n! for an integer represents the factorial function n!=n⋅(n−1)⋅(n−2)⋯2⋅1n!=n⋅(n−1)⋅(n−2)⋯2⋅1.
  • loglog, sinsin, coscos are generally in math mode.
  • Fractions may be represented using abab or inline using a/ba/b.
  • |x||x| represents the absolute value of xx, eg, |−2|=2|−2|=2.
  • Standard geometric notation is used such as the triangle △ABC△ABC with sides ABAB, BCBC, and ACAC. Standard notation is also used for angles, eg, ∠ABC∠ABC. Angles may be specified in degrees ∠ABC=60∘∠ABC=60∘.
  • Standard trigonometric operators including sinsin, coscos, tantan may be used.
  • Sets are denoted using standard curly bracket notation, eg, {1,2,3}{1,2,3}.
  • ⌊x⌋⌊x⌋ represents the integer part of the real number xx ie the greatest integer less than or equal to xx. ⌈x⌉⌈x⌉ represents the smallest integer greater than or equal to xx. {x}{x} for a single value xx refers to the fractional part of the real number xx ie x−⌊x⌋x−⌊x⌋.
  • \left\left and \right\right may be used in conjunction with other LaTeX commands eg \left \lceil \frac{x}{2}\right\rceil\left \lceil \frac{x}{2}\right\rceil producing ⌈x2⌉⌈x2⌉.
  • Overline notation abcd¯¯¯¯¯¯¯¯¯¯abcd¯ represents the integer formed by the digits aa,bb,cc,d∈{0,1,…,9}d∈{0,1,…,9} with a≠0a≠0 eg with a=1a=1, b=2b=2: ab¯¯¯¯¯=12ab¯=12 and bab¯¯¯¯¯¯¯=212bab¯=212.
  • (ab)(ab) is interpreted as the binomial coefficient, representing the number of ways of choosing bb elements from a set of size aa.
  • N,Z,QN,Z,Q, and RR are used to denote the set of positive integers, integers, rational numbers, and real numbers, respectively (where positive means >0>0).
  • We allow informal probability language such as: a point is chosen uniformly at random in the interval [0,1][0,1].
  • The sum over the empty set is 00, and the product over the empty set is 11.
  • For integers ll, mm, and nn, lmnlmn denotes l(mn)l(mn).
  • British or American versions of English can be used. Thus ''highest common factor'' means the same as ''greatest common divisor''.
  • A prefix or subscript may be used to indicate features of a triangle associated with vertices. Thus triangle ABCABC has three altitudes, and the one dropped from AA could be denoted the altitude through AA, the AA-altitude or the altitude haha. Similarly, for median lines.
  • :=:= means 'is defined to be equal to'.
  • Note on taxonomy: An equilateral triangle is a specific type of isosceles triangle, a square is a specific type of rectangle, and a rectangle is a specific type of parallelogram, among other similar relationships.
  • The word trapezium can mean different things in different countries. For the purposes of this competition, a trapezium is a quadrilateral with at least one pair of parallel opposite sides.
